# The Story Guide — Every World Needs a Story

This engine can teach anything. But nobody remembers a reading list — they
remember the trail, the winter, and who died at the river crossing. Before a
single lesson is authored, the world needs a story, and this guide is how an
AI builder (or a human one) helps a creator find it.

**How to use this:** answer the eight questions below, in order, in writing.
Each has a test. If an answer fails its test, stop and fix it before moving
on — everything downstream (lessons, events, Echelon's voice, the grade)
inherits from these answers. The worked example throughout is How to Save
the World itself (see `WORLD_DESIGN.md` for its full design).

---

## 1. The Trail — what journey is the learner on?

Not "what will they learn" — what are they *doing*? A trail has a direction,
terrain, and a reason the learner (not the creator) wants to reach the end.

- *HTSTW:* the transition from a centralized digital world to a
  decentralized one, walked mission by mission.
- **Test:** can you say it as "you are traveling from ___ to ___" without
  using the word "curriculum"?

## 2. The Winter — what happens if the learner does nothing?

The Oregon Trail had winter; entropy needs a direction. Something in your
world must get worse *by default*, so that showing up matters.

- *HTSTW:* The Slide — the world drifts toward centralization on its own;
  every mission is a chance to push back.
- **Test:** if the learner stalls for ten missions, what has visibly
  degraded? If the answer is "nothing," you have a syllabus, not a world.

## 3. The Adversaries — who profits from the drift? Name them.

Abstract dangers teach nothing. Give the opposing forces names, faces,
institutions, and — critically — **genuinely tempting offers**. Each
adversary personifies one real failure mode of your domain and one flavor
of scarcity logic ("there isn't enough ___ to go around — let us hold it").

- *HTSTW:* Meridian Trust (custody), the Ministry of Convenience (exit
  costs), Aperture (surveillance), the Custodians (gatekept AI).
- **Test:** would a smart, busy person take each adversary's deal? If no
  offer tempts *you*, it's a strawman, and strawmen teach nothing. (The
  ferry really was safer. It just cost the five dollars you needed for the
  mountains.)

## 4. The Companion — who walks beside the learner?

The engine gives every learner an AI companion. Decide who it *is*: name,
voice, loyalty, limits, and how it addresses the learner. The companion is
the interface; its consistency is the immersion.

- *HTSTW:* Echelon — training intelligence in the Foxhole; calls the
  learner "Operator"; teaches by drills and debriefs, never lectures past
  the fog.
- **Test:** write three sentences your companion would say and one it never
  would. If you can't produce the "never," it has no character yet.

## 5. The Stakes — what is scarce, and what is abundant?

Decide what the learner's finite resource represents (attention, trust,
signal, credibility) and what regenerates it. Keep one rule sacred: the
learner can always earn more *by doing the work* — scarcity of the resource
is self-inflicted, never sold back to them.

- *HTSTW:* Signal — spent by rushing and convenience bargains, generated by
  reflection and honest drills.
- **Test:** name the resource, one way it drains, one way it regenerates —
  and confirm none of the three involves payment.

## 6. The Consequences — what does failure teach?

Choices must be able to go wrong, but random death teaches nothing real.
Design the setback ladder: detours (re-run at higher difficulty), holds
(something of the learner's is kept by an adversary until recovered), and
the deep failure state — capture, not death. The learner always comes back;
they come back with a temporary handicap and a permanent scar the companion
remembers.

- *HTSTW:* the robot prisons — Aperture's Observation Wing, the Custodians'
  Alignment Facility. Recovery is itself a lesson; the scar becomes a story.
- **Test:** for each failure, complete the sentence "getting out of this
  teaches ___." If nothing fills the blank, the failure is cruelty, not
  pedagogy.

## 7. The Reports — how does the world mirror the learner back?

At intervals, the world should tell the learner who they are becoming — a
grade of *judgment, not activity*, delivered in-fiction with receipts
(decisions cited, scars honored, recoveries commended). Never score speed,
volume, or streaks; grinding must be worthless.

- *HTSTW:* Commander's Reports at section boundaries; rating bands
  PROVISIONAL → STEADY → TRUSTED → EXEMPLARY COMMAND; archived in the
  dossier.
- **Test:** could a learner improve their rating by doing *more* without
  thinking *better*? If yes, redesign it.

## 8. The Destination — what world do they graduate into?

The ending should be read off the world's state — the learner graduates
into the world their choices built, and the ceremony says so honestly.
Multiple endings give the campaign weight from the first act.

- *HTSTW:* graduate under Open Sky and it's the full ceremony; graduate in
  Locktown and the companion signs off under surveillance — then: "Run it
  again, Operator. The trail is still there."
- **Test:** describe two different endings a learner could earn. If there's
  only one, the meter was decoration.

# Part Two — Weaving the Story Into Every Lesson

The eight questions build the world. This part is where most worlds still
fail: the bible is rich, the lessons stay generic. A learner can complete
five missions in a well-designed world and never once be told why *they*
are there or what the concept in front of them has to do with the war.
These four rules prevent that. Audit every generated lesson against them.

## 9. The Recruitment Contract — say it, don't imply it

The learner must be told, in-fiction and early, three things: **how they
were recruited**, **why them** (what aptitude the assessment saw, and what
role that earns them in the effort), and **what end the work serves**. This
is not a one-time cutscene — briefings restate it whenever it sharpens the
stakes. On this engine, the personalized role lives in the world context
the companion receives every message (see `campaign-engine.ts`
`buildWorldPromptContext`); authored lesson text stays role-agnostic and
the companion personalizes at delivery.

- *HTSTW:* "Your assessment flagged the Watchtower pattern. You serve as
  Risk & Security Analyst. The Vanguard is building LATTICE before the
  Slide locks autonomy into few hands."
- **Test:** could a learner three missions in answer "why were you
  recruited, and for what?" in one sentence? If not, the contract was
  implied, not stated.

## 10. The Mission Link — mechanism, not sentiment

Every lesson's briefing must name the **concrete causal mechanism** by
which this concept serves the campaign — never "this matters to the
mission." For latency: *systems that sense and respond locally don't wait
on a distant controller; every second of lag is an argument someone will
use for handing control to whoever owns the fastest pipe.*

- **Test (the swap test):** replace the concept's name in the mission-link
  sentence with a different concept. If the sentence still reads fine, it
  was decoration — rewrite until only *this* concept fits.

## 11. The Applied Turn — the learner thinks about something real

A framework is not learned until it has been run against the learner's own
reality. Every framework the lessons teach must be *walked*, not shown:
the companion first asks the learner to name something real they are
working on, then asks the framework's questions against that case one at a
time — the learner answers each, the companion sharpens and probes, and
never answers for them. On this engine this is enforced in the stage
instructions (HP and drill stages, `echelon-chat`), but authored drill
prompts should invite it too.

- *HTSTW:* the Monkey's Paw Rule isn't recited — the operator names a
  live project and answers "what might this solve / break / harm?" about
  *it*, with Echelon pressing on each answer.
- **Test:** count the questions in a mission the learner can answer
  without referencing their own life or work. If they can pass the whole
  mission in the abstract, it teaches trivia.

## 12. The Beat — every lesson carries one thread of the arc

Each lesson gets a `story_beat`: one or two sentences of campaign
continuity — what moved in the world since last mission, what this mission
touches. The companion weaves it into the briefing (never as a synopsis).
Beats are narrative, not schema tokens; a beat that reads like
`boundary_map; system_scope_principle` is corrupted data, not story.

- **Test:** read five consecutive beats aloud. They should feel like one
  campaign advancing, not five taglines.

### The briefing anatomy (the shape that passed review)

Two short paragraphs of authored text, which the companion delivers and
personalizes:

1. **TEACH** — what the concept *is*, plainly, no unexplained jargon, plus
   the mission-link mechanism (rule 10).
2. **ORIENT** — recruitment frame (rule 9, role-agnostic), the story beat
   (rule 12), and what this mission will ask — ending, where real, with
   the learner applying it to their own work (rule 11).

## The Creator's Worksheet

Copy, fill, and keep with the fork. An AI builder should refuse to
mass-generate lessons until this is filled in.

```
WORLD NAME:
1. THE TRAIL      — you are traveling from ______ to ______
2. THE WINTER     — left alone, the world ______
3. THE ADVERSARIES — name / vector / tempting offer (×2 minimum)
4. THE COMPANION  — name / voice / one thing it would never say
5. THE STAKES     — resource / what drains it / what regenerates it
6. THE CONSEQUENCES — detour: ____  hold: ____  capture: ____
7. THE REPORTS    — interval / rating bands / what is never scored
8. THE DESTINATION — best ending / hardest ending

PER-LESSON WEAVE (audit every generated lesson):
9.  RECRUITMENT CONTRACT — recruited how / why them / toward what end
10. MISSION LINK  — concept → campaign mechanism (passes the swap test)
11. APPLIED TURN  — where the learner runs it against their own work
12. THE BEAT      — one thread of the arc, in narrative, not tokens
```

A world with eight honest answers is ready for lessons. A world without them
is a reading list wearing a costume. And lessons that skip the weave rules
are a reading list *inside* a costume — the story stays on the box art and
never reaches the learner's hands.
